73% of Dogs Have Dental Disease Right Now — Why Oral Care Is Your Most Overlooked Category

📅 June 23, 2026 ✍️ PetStore.com Editorial Team 🏷️ Wellness · Merchandising · Margins

According to 2024 Banfield Pet Hospital data covering more than 3 million pets, 73% of dogs and 64% of cats seen by vets had a diagnosed dental issue. By their third birthday, 80% of dogs and up to 90% of cats already show signs of periodontal disease. And yet walk through most independent pet stores — online or off — and oral care gets one cramped shelf between the nail clippers and the bandanas. That gap between "almost every pet has this problem" and "almost no store merchandises the fix" is exactly where your next high-margin category is hiding.

The Numbers Nobody's Talking About

The global pet oral care products market is projected to more than double to $26.5 billion by 2032, growing at a 10.6% CAGR — one of the fastest-growing segments in the entire pet category. Dental chews and treats alone already capture 41.3% of the market, and oral probiotic tablets are forecast to grow at 7.4% CAGR through 2031 as pet owners look for options beyond chewing.

The health stakes make this more than a nice-to-have. Dogs with advanced periodontal disease are 2.3x more likely to develop chronic kidney disease and 6.3x more likely to develop endocarditis — a heart valve infection. Cats with advanced periodontal disease are 1.5x more likely to develop chronic kidney disease. Vets know this. Pet owners increasingly know this too. They just don't know what to buy, and that's your opening.

VOHC Is the Only Filter That Actually Matters

Here's what separates real oral care products from marketing fluff: the Veterinary Oral Health Council (VOHC) seal. It's awarded only to products with clinical trial data proving they reduce plaque or tartar — and it's the single credibility marker pet owners trust enough to pay a premium for.

This isn't a small distinction for your margins. Brands with third-party VOHC validation can justify meaningfully higher shelf prices than unverified "dental" treats that are really just chew toys with a marketing claim slapped on. If your product pages don't call out VOHC status front and center, you're leaving trust — and margin — on the table.

The SKUs Actually Worth Stocking

You don't need 40 dental SKUs. You need a tight, credible lineup across price points and formats:

  • Greenies — clinically shown to cut tartar by 47% and plaque by 40% in 28 days. The category leader for a reason.
  • Purina DentaLife — VOHC-approved with an average 57% tartar reduction in manufacturer trials. Strong mid-tier price point.
  • Virbac VeggieDent FR3SH — its Z-shape design and enzyme system report up to 70% tartar reduction with extended use. Premium positioning that justifies premium pricing.
  • WHIMZEES — the grain-free, VOHC-accepted pick for owners avoiding fillers, a fast-growing customer segment.
  • Milk-Bone Brushing Chews — one of the few genuinely affordable options that still carries legitimate VOHC approval, useful for price-sensitive shoppers you don't want to lose entirely.

Round that out with one water additive and one enzymatic toothpaste-and-finger-brush kit, and you've covered every entry point — chewers, non-chewers, and owners who actually want to brush.

Stop Burying It — Build It Into a Real Category

Oral care suffers the same fate in most stores as supplements did five years ago: scattered across "treats," "health," and "accessories" with no coherent path to purchase. Pull it into its own dedicated category page. Lead with the stat that gets attention — "80% of dogs have dental disease by age 3" — then segment by chewer type (aggressive, moderate, gentle/senior) the way you'd segment shoes by size.

Grooming and oral care products carry 40–50% margins with the kind of steady, repeat demand that makes them ideal for a featured homepage slot, not a buried subcategory. A customer who lands on a clearly organized dental page with VOHC badges and a "which chew fits my dog's chew style" quiz converts at a completely different rate than one scrolling past a generic "treats" grid.

Dental Care Is Recurring Revenue Hiding in Plain Sight

Dental chews are consumable, used daily, and run out predictably — which makes this category a natural fit for subscription. Around 48% of U.S. pet owners already use some form of autoship, and a monthly dental chew refill is one of the easiest "set it and forget it" subscriptions to sell, because the alternative — remembering to reorder before your dog's chews run out — is genuinely annoying for the customer.

Bundle a 30-day chew supply with a water additive and toothpaste kit into a single "Complete Oral Care" autoship box. It's an easy upsell at checkout, and because it solves a recurring problem rather than a one-time purchase, retention on dental bundles tends to run higher than on standalone treat purchases.

Content That Actually Converts: Skip the Sales Pitch, Lead with Education

Nobody searches "buy dog dental chews" because they're excited about dental chews. They search because a vet mentioned tartar at the last checkup, or their dog's breath got noticeably worse. Write the content that meets them there: "Why does my dog's breath suddenly smell bad?" or "Periodontal disease in small dog breeds — why Chihuahuas and Dachshunds are at higher risk." Small breeds under 14.3 pounds are up to five times more likely to develop periodontal disease than giant breeds — a genuinely surprising, shareable stat that most owners of small dogs have never heard.

Pair that content with short video — a 30-second clip showing how to introduce a finger brush to a resistant dog outperforms any product photo. This is a category built for trust-based selling, and trust-based selling is the one advantage independent stores have over Chewy and Amazon.

Pet Dental Health Month Is Your Built-In Marketing Calendar

February is National Pet Dental Health Month, but you don't need to wait until next year — use it as a template for a recurring quarterly push. Run a "free dental check-in" content series, partner with a local vet for a co-branded guide, or simply re-promote your oral care bundle every few months with fresh before/after testimonials. The category has a built-in marketing hook most stores never use more than once a year.

Build the Category Other Stores Are Ignoring

This is a rare setup: a problem affecting nearly three-quarters of pets, a credible third-party certification system that makes trust easy to signal, consumable products that beg for subscription, and competitors who are still treating the whole thing as an afterthought aisle. That's not a crowded market — that's an open lane.

Start small. Pick five VOHC-approved SKUs, give them a real category page, and bundle them into an autoship box. The dogs (and their kidneys) will thank you.
