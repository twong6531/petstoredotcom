EPR Packaging Laws Just Became Your Problem — Here's How to Get Ahead of Them

📅 June 21, 2026 ✍️ PetStore.com Editorial Team 🏷️ Strategy · Sustainability · Shipping

Seven states — Maine, Oregon, California, Colorado, Minnesota, Maryland, and Washington — now have Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) packaging laws on the books, with Maine's program already fully operational and Oregon, California, and Colorado phasing in fees and reporting through 2027. If you ship pet food, treats, or litter to customers in any of those states, this isn't a someday problem. It's a this-year problem, and most independent pet store owners have no idea it's coming.

What EPR Actually Means for a Pet Store

EPR shifts the cost of packaging waste from municipalities onto the businesses that put packaging into the market. In practice, that means producers and, in many states, the retailers and brand owners selling into that state, must register with a Producer Responsibility Organization, report the weight and material type of every package they ship, and pay fees scaled to recyclability. A 30-pound bag of kibble in a non-recyclable multi-layer pouch costs you more under these laws than the same product in a mono-material, curbside-recyclable bag.

Right now, at least 11 more states have EPR bills moving through 2026 legislative sessions — Georgia, Illinois, Massachusetts, Missouri, Nebraska, New Hampshire, New Jersey, New York, Rhode Island, Tennessee, and Wisconsin. This is not a regional quirk. It's the new baseline for ecommerce packaging in the U.S., the same way GDPR became the baseline for data handling.

Why Pet Products Are Exposed More Than Most Categories

Pet food and litter packaging is uniquely bad from a recyclability standpoint. The Pet Sustainability Coalition estimates that 300 million pounds of pet food and treat bags are generated in the U.S. every year, and over 99% of those bags are never recycled — they're multi-layer laminates designed for barrier performance, not for any municipal recycling stream. If you're a private-label seller or you source from smaller manufacturers still using legacy laminate pouches, you're sitting on exactly the packaging profile these laws are designed to penalize.

Direct-to-consumer shipping makes it worse. DHL and other logistics research groups have flagged that ecommerce packaging takes significantly more abuse than retail shelf packaging — more seal failures, more burst risk — which has historically pushed brands toward heavier, harder-to-recycle barrier films. That's the exact tradeoff EPR fee structures are now pricing in.

The Brands Already Solving This (Steal Their Playbook)

Mars switched its Whiskas pet food pouches in the UK and Germany to a mono-material polypropylene structure that's heat-resistant enough for sterilization and fully recyclable — and cut the packaging's carbon footprint by 46% in the process. TC Transcontinental's VieVERTe line is being prequalified by How2Recycle in the U.S. specifically for pet food applications, using BOPE (biaxially oriented polyethylene) film that's thinner, stronger, and mono-material. EVOH-free barrier technology now achieves oxygen transmission rates under 2 cc/m²/day — performance that matches old-school laminates without the recycling penalty.

The practical takeaway: when you're sourcing or renegotiating with suppliers this year, ask explicitly whether their pouches are mono-PE or mono-PP and whether they're How2Recycle certified. If a supplier can't answer that question, that's your signal to start shopping.

Set Up a TerraCycle Drop-Off Program — It's Free and It's a Marketing Asset

Pet Supplies Plus and Wag N' Wash partnered with TerraCycle in 2023 to run free in-store recycling for any brand of pet food, treat, or litter bag, and have since diverted more than 30 tons of packaging from landfills. Wellness, Royal Canin, and Earthborn Holistic all run brand-specific free TerraCycle programs for their own packaging.

You don't need a physical storefront to use this. Offer customers a prepaid TerraCycle mailer for hard-to-recycle pouches as a loyalty perk, or partner directly with one of the brand-specific programs (Wellness, Royal Canin, Earthborn) if you carry their products — it costs you nothing and gives you a genuine sustainability story instead of a vague "eco-friendly" claim that 67% of shoppers under 40 have learned to ignore.

Fix Your Outbound Shipping Before the Audits Start

EPR fee structures don't stop at the product package — your outbound shipping box or mailer counts too in most state frameworks. This is also where the easiest savings are. Right-sizing your shipping boxes can cut shipping costs 20-30% immediately, and it's the single fastest way to reduce reportable packaging weight at the same time. If you're still shipping a 12-ounce bag of treats in a box built for a 20-pound bag of kibble, you're paying twice: once in dimensional shipping fees, once in EPR-reportable material weight.

  • Audit your top 20 SKUs — measure actual product dimensions against the boxes/mailers you're using today
  • Switch to compostable or recycled-content mailers for soft goods (treats, toys, small accessories) — these typically qualify for reduced EPR fee tiers
  • Consolidate carrier pickups to weekly or bi-weekly batches — shippers report 18-30% reductions in handling and fuel surcharges from this alone

Do You Even Need to Register? Here's the Quick Test

EPR laws generally apply to "producers" — which most state frameworks define broadly enough to include private-label sellers and, in some cases, retailers who are the first point of sale into the state. If you sell branded third-party products only and ship in volumes below each state's small-business threshold (Maine and Oregon both exempt businesses under certain revenue and tonnage minimums), you may be exempt for now. But thresholds are typically reviewed annually and tend to tighten, not loosen, as programs mature. The move that actually protects you: track your packaging weight and material types by state destination starting now, even before you're legally required to report. You'll need that data eventually, and reconstructing two years of shipping history under deadline pressure is a much worse position than having it ready.

Turn Compliance Into a Competitive Advantage

Chewy and Amazon operate at a scale where EPR compliance is a line item buried in a much bigger logistics operation. You're small enough to actually tell your customers what you're doing about it — and pet owners increasingly care. Ethical and sustainability claims are rising fastest among younger pet owners specifically, and "we ship in fully recyclable, mono-material packaging" is a sentence that builds trust in a way "free shipping" never will, because it's specific and verifiable rather than generic.

Put a short, honest packaging page on your site. List exactly what's recyclable, what's compostable, and what isn't yet (be honest about the "not yet" — it builds more credibility than pretending everything is solved). This single page does double duty: it differentiates you from big-box retailers and it documents your compliance posture if a state ever asks.

The Bottom Line

EPR packaging laws are moving from "interesting policy news" to "operational requirement" faster than most independent retailers have noticed. The stores that get ahead of it now — auditing packaging, switching suppliers to mono-material formats, setting up free recycling partnerships, right-sizing shipping — will absorb the new fee structures as a rounding error. The stores that wait will be scrambling through compliance audits with two years of unsorted shipping data and a packaging mix designed for a regulatory environment that no longer exists.
