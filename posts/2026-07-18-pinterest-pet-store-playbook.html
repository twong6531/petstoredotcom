75% of Pinners Are Already Shopping. Here's Why Your Pet Store Isn't — And the 2026 Playbook to Fix It.

📅 July 18, 2026 ✍️ PetStore.com Editorial Team 🏷️ Pinterest · Marketing · Social Commerce

Here's a number that should rearrange your marketing priorities: 96% of searches on Pinterest have no brand name in them. That means when someone searches "best freeze-dried dog food" or "calming supplements for anxious cats," they're not looking for Chewy, Purina, or Amazon. They're open to whoever shows up first — and that could be you.

Meanwhile, most independent pet stores are pouring time and budget into TikTok, Instagram, and Meta ads while ignoring a platform where 75% of weekly users are already in active purchase mode — nearly three times the rate on Facebook. Pinterest isn't a social media platform. It's a visual search engine that people use when they've already decided to buy something. And right now, it is profoundly underused by indie pet stores.

Let's fix that.

Why Pinterest Isn't TikTok (And Why That's a Good Thing for Your Revenue)

TikTok is discovery. Pinterest is decision. That's the most important distinction in all of social commerce.

When someone finds your brand on TikTok, they're entertained — and maybe they'll buy. When someone finds your product on Pinterest, they're planning a purchase. Pinterest's own data shows that its purchase intent index sits at 5.6x the social platform average. Users have 2.2x higher purchase intent than on any other social channel.

The practical implication: you need far fewer impressions to drive a sale on Pinterest than anywhere else. Brands running Pinterest Shopping ads report an average ecommerce ROAS in the 3.2x–4.8x range, with Shopping ads specifically delivering 2.6x higher conversion rates than standard pins. And unlike a TikTok video that disappears from the algorithm in 72 hours, a well-optimized Pinterest pin can drive traffic and sales for months or years.

Pinterest now has 537 million monthly active users globally (up from 498M last year) and the user base skews heavily toward 25–54-year-old women — the demographic that makes the majority of household pet purchasing decisions in the U.S. This is not a coincidence. It's your audience.

Pet Products Are Built for Pinterest

Pinterest is a visual search engine that rewards beautiful, specific, aspirational imagery. Think about what that means for pet products:

  • A raw dog food bowl styled like a meal prep photo
  • An enrichment puzzle set up in a clean living room
  • A lick mat in a bathtub with a nervous dog mid-groom
  • Before/after photos of a dog's coat on a fish oil supplement
  • A senior cat on a heated orthopedic bed beside a reading lamp

Pet content consistently performs in Pinterest's highest-traffic categories: Home & Living, DIY & Crafts, Health & Wellness, and Food & Drinks (which includes pet nutrition). Products with visual appeal, lifestyle context, and a clear benefit claim are exactly what Pinterest's algorithm rewards. Your freeze-dried toppers, calming chews, puzzle feeders, and cooling mats are perfectly positioned — you just need to show them in the right context.

The Unbranded Search Opportunity Nobody's Talking About

Let's revisit that 96% unbranded search stat, because it's extraordinary.

On Google, competing for pet food terms is brutal. Chewy and Petco have domain authority you can't touch. On Pinterest, those same searches — "raw dog food for small breeds," "best calming treats for cats," "natural flea prevention 2026" — are wide open. Nobody owns them. Pinterest's SEO is a keyword game just like Google's, except the competition is minimal and the barrier to entry is zero.

The strategy: build keyword-optimized boards and pin descriptions around your actual product categories. Board names like "Raw + Freeze-Dried Dog Food," "Calming Supplements for Dogs," and "Senior Cat Wellness" will appear in Pinterest search results and pull in high-intent shoppers who have never heard of your store. This is free, organic traffic from buyers who are already shopping. You just have to show up.

The best part: 85% of weekly Pinterest users have made a purchase based on a brand pin they saw on the platform. Every pin you post is a conversion opportunity that compounds over time.

Set Up Your Pinterest Shop Catalog First (Takes One Afternoon)

Before you pin a single lifestyle photo, connect your product catalog. This is the foundation of everything else.

If you're on Shopify, the Pinterest app syncs your full catalog automatically in about 20 minutes. Once your catalog is connected, every product pin becomes a Product Rich Pin — it pulls in live pricing, availability, and a direct "Buy" link that updates automatically when you change anything in your store. No manual updating, no broken links, no outdated prices.

From your catalog you can create Collections — curated product groupings that show as a single pin with a shoppable grid underneath. A "Summer Cooling Essentials" collection pin or "Digestive Health Starter Kit" collection is an impulse-buy machine for shoppers already in research mode.

Enable Pinterest Tag on your website at the same time. It's the equivalent of the Meta Pixel — it tracks which products people view and purchase so you can retarget them with ads and build lookalike audiences from your best buyers. This one tag, set up once, will pay for itself in better ad targeting for as long as you run Pinterest ads.

Your Pinterest Content Calendar: What to Pin and When

Pinterest rewards consistency more than frequency. Aim for 5–10 new pins per day — but don't panic, because fresh pins doesn't mean fresh photography. Mix content types:

  • Product pins — pulled directly from your catalog, with keyword-rich descriptions (150–300 characters, include the product name, key ingredients, and a clear benefit)
  • Educational infographics — "Signs Your Dog's Gut Health Is Off" or "Raw vs. Kibble: What the Labels Don't Tell You" — these get saved and reshared constantly
  • Lifestyle imagery — your products in real home environments, not studio white backgrounds
  • Video pins — 15–60 seconds, product demos, unboxings, or before/after results
  • Idea Pins (formerly Story Pins) — step-by-step guides like "How to Transition Your Dog to Raw Food in 7 Days"

Seasonal content is Pinterest's superpower. Pinterest users plan purchases 30–60 days in advance — meaning your "Back to School Pet Anxiety" content performs best in July, not September. Your holiday gift guides should go up in October. Your summer cooling content should have been live in April. Think ahead and you'll own the seasonal slots before the algorithm even warms up.

Pinterest Ads: High Intent, Lower CPC Than You'd Expect

Pinterest ads are dramatically underpriced relative to the purchase intent they deliver. While Meta CPC for pet products averages around $0.61, Pinterest Shopping ads typically run at comparable or lower CPCs — but convert at 2.6x the rate. The math on cost per acquisition is hard to ignore.

The most effective ad format for pet stores is the Shopping Campaign, which pulls directly from your product catalog and serves ads to users searching for related terms. You set a daily budget (start at $15–30/day to gather data), choose which product groups to promote, and Pinterest's algorithm does the targeting. No audience building required — it finds buyers for you.

Retargeting is where the real money is. Once your Pinterest Tag has 90+ days of data, build a retargeting campaign targeting users who viewed product pages but didn't purchase. These are warm leads — they found you organically, they're interested, and a well-timed product pin is often the last push they need. Retargeting campaigns on Pinterest consistently outperform prospecting by 30–50% on ROAS.

Video Pins Are Your Secret Weapon

Here's a specific data point worth acting on immediately: a pet groomer in Chicago replaced their static photo ads on Pinterest with 15-second before/after grooming video clips. Their click-through rate jumped from 3% to 8% and their cost per booking dropped 28%. Pet video content — a dog's reaction to a new treat, a cat investigating a puzzle feeder, a lick mat in action — performs dramatically better than static product images.

You don't need production quality. You need authenticity and a clear hook in the first two seconds. Film your dog going absolutely berserk for your best-selling freeze-dried treat and post it as a 20-second Pinterest video pin. Write a description that includes the product name, the key ingredient, and what problem it solves. Add a direct link to the product page. That's the whole formula.

The kicker: Pinterest video pins live indefinitely in search results. That 20-second clip you film today can drive traffic three years from now. TikTok videos have a 48-hour shelf life at best. This is a fundamentally different ROI calculus — and it strongly favors Pinterest for stores that think about long-term marketing efficiency.

The Pinterest-to-Autoship Pipeline

Pinterest users plan. They save things to buy later. They build boards called "Dog Essentials" and "What My Cat Needs." These are not impulse shoppers — they're considered buyers who research before they purchase, and they come back to those saved pins again and again.

This behavior maps perfectly to autoship conversion. When a user saves your freeze-dried topper or digestive supplement pin, they're signaling deep purchase intent. Send them to a product page that leads with your autoship option — "Subscribe and save 15%: delivered on your schedule, cancel anytime" — and you'll convert a Pinterest saver into a monthly recurring customer.

Add a "Pinterest exclusive" offer to high-intent product pages: a first-time autoship discount, a free sample bundle on subscription orders over $50, or early access to new products for autoship members. Measure click-through from your Pinterest tag against autoship signups in your analytics. You'll find that Pinterest traffic, while lower volume than Meta, converts to autoship subscribers at a meaningfully higher rate — because these are buyers who already know what they want.

Your Pinterest Week-One Checklist

Start here and don't overthink it:

  • Day 1: Install the Pinterest app on Shopify, connect your catalog, enable the Pinterest Tag
  • Day 2: Create 5 keyword-optimized boards around your core product categories
  • Day 3: Pin 10 products from your catalog with keyword-rich descriptions
  • Day 4: Create one educational infographic (Canva has free templates)
  • Day 5: Film one 15–20 second video of a pet interacting with your best-selling product
  • Week 2: Launch a $20/day Pinterest Shopping campaign on your top 5 products

That's it. Six days of setup to open a channel that your competitors almost certainly haven't touched — and one that connects directly to the customers most likely to become your highest-value autoship subscribers.

Pinterest is the only platform where your content compounds over time, your audience is already shopping, and 96% of the search landscape has no brand owner. That combination doesn't exist anywhere else. Build the presence now, before your competitors figure it out.
