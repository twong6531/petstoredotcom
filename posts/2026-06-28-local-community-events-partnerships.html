91% of Shoppers Buy Local Every Week — Why Your Online Pet Store Still Needs a Community Strategy

📅 June 28, 2026 ✍️ PetStore.com Editorial Team 🏷️ Marketing · Community · Pet Industry

Here's the stat that should make every online-only pet store owner stop and rethink their marketing budget: 91% of U.S. consumers shop at a small or local business in any given week, and 87% say they buy a brand's products after attending one of its in-person events. You've spent the last two years building funnels, flows, and feeds. Meanwhile, your customers are standing in a park on a Saturday morning, with their dog, looking for exactly the kind of business you run — and you're nowhere to be found. Showing up in person isn't a distraction from your ecommerce strategy. Done right, it's the highest-converting top-of-funnel you have.

Why "Online-Only" Doesn't Mean "Invisible Locally"

Running your store online doesn't mean your customers live online. They live in a neighborhood, walk their dog at the same park every morning, and belong to the same Facebook groups and Nextdoor threads as everyone else. The data on why people choose to shop small backs this up: 49% cite convenience and location, 45% cite product quality, 43% cite price, and 41% say they specifically want to support a local business. None of those reasons require you to have a storefront — they require you to be present.

And the dollars matter too: at least $68 of every $100 spent at a local business stays in that community, recirculating through other small businesses, schools, and services. When you sponsor a shelter's adoption day or set up a booth at the farmers market, you're not just advertising — you're making a deposit into a local economy that remembers who showed up.

The Adoption-Day Playbook: Borrow What Petco Love and BISSELL Already Built

You don't need to invent the wheel here — national programs have already proven the model and you can plug into it locally. Petco Love (with Hill's) has helped place roughly 7 million animals through partner adoption events, typically bundling retail tie-ins like discounted supplies or a free starter bag of food at the point of adoption. BISSELL Pet Foundation's "Empty the Shelters" is the largest funded adoption event in the country — active across 49 states, having donated more than $28 million since 2011 and surpassed 1 million pets placed in mid-2025.

Call your local shelter or rescue and ask to be the retail sponsor for their next reduced-fee adoption weekend. Offer every new adopter a starter kit — a small bag of food, a leash, a coupon code — and capture their email at pickup. That single Saturday gives you a room full of brand-new pet owners who need everything, at the exact moment they're most receptive to a recommendation.

Pop-Ups Are Cheaper Than You Think — Here's the Real Cost

Farmers markets and weekend pop-ups are the lowest-risk way to test in-person presence. Most markets charge $20–$75 per week for a vendor booth, with high-traffic urban markets running $100+ a day; many offer seasonal packages of 20–25 weeks for $600–$1,250 total, which works out cheaper than paying week to week. A basic pop-up tent setup runs roughly $150–$250, and if you want liability coverage for the season, budget another $300–$500/year.

That's a total startup cost well under $1,000 to test whether a local market converts foot traffic into customers — a fraction of what most stores spend testing a new paid ad channel, and with a much higher trust payoff since people are meeting you face to face.

Stack Cross-Promotions With Vets, Trainers, and Groomers

The cheapest events to run are the ones you don't run alone. A joint "Pet Care Day" with a local vet clinic, a free training demo with a certified trainer, or a grooming clinic booth co-hosted with a mobile groomer splits your costs and pools two audiences instead of one. Experiential marketing data backs this up directly: 65% of brands report a direct correlation between sales and their experiential marketing efforts, and with two or three partners sharing the table, your cost per lead drops accordingly.

Reach out to three local pet-adjacent businesses this month — a vet, a trainer, a groomer — and propose a single co-branded Saturday event. You bring the retail products and the raffle prizes; they bring the expertise and their client list.

Make Every Event an Email-Capture Machine

None of this matters if you don't convert the foot traffic into a list you own. Email remains the highest-ROI channel in marketing, returning roughly $36 for every $1 spent — which makes a QR code on your booth, your tote bags, and your raffle entry form one of the most valuable pieces of signage you'll print all year. Set up a simple "spin to win" or raffle entry that requires an email and a pet's name, and route every new signup straight into a welcome flow that introduces your autoship program within 48 hours.

Treat every event the same way you'd treat a paid ad campaign: set a target number of emails captured, track it, and calculate cost per lead. A $700 farmers market season that nets 400 qualified local leads is outperforming most Meta ad campaigns running right now.

Turn Showing Up Into Showing Up Again — Pickup Days

If you don't have a storefront, create a recurring "pickup day" — a monthly or biweekly window where local customers can collect online orders in person at a partner location (a co-working space, a dog park pavilion, the same farmers market booth) instead of paying for shipping. It gives local customers a reason to choose you over Chewy on price (free local pickup beats a $6 shipping fee), and it gives you a recurring, predictable touchpoint to build real relationships instead of one-off events.

Promote pickup days through the email list you're already building from your events — this is the flywheel: event → email → pickup day → repeat purchase → autoship.

The Bottom Line

Chewy and Amazon cannot show up at your local farmers market, sponsor your shelter's adoption weekend, or co-host a training demo with the trainer half your customers already use. That is entirely your lane. With 80% of consumers saying they shop locally specifically to support their community, and experiential marketing converting at rates digital ads can only dream of, a few hundred dollars and a weekend a month can build a local reputation that compounds the way SEO and paid social never will.

Pick one: a shelter adoption-day sponsorship, a farmers market season, or a co-branded vet/trainer event. Run it once this summer, capture every email, and measure what it does to your repeat purchase rate over the next 90 days.
