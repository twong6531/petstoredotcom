Gut Health Is the New Battleground in Pet Food — Here's How to Stock the Winning Category

📅 June 17, 2026 ✍️ PetStore.com Editorial Team 🏷️ Nutrition · Supplements · Pet Industry

In 2025, 31% of all new pet food launches carried a digestive or gut health claim. Prebiotic ingredient use in pet food grew 63% annually over the last five years. Postbiotics — the newest, hottest ingredient class — grew 150% annually over the same stretch. If your shelves still treat "digestive support" as a single SKU buried next to the hairball remedies, you're sitting on the fastest-moving subcategory in pet nutrition and not cashing in on it.

Why Gut Health Became the Industry's Obsession

This isn't a fad cooked up by a supplement brand's marketing team. Pet owners rank digestive wellness as one of their top four health concerns for their animals, right alongside skin health, weight management, and behavior — and it's the one they can see every single day. A loose stool or dull coat is an immediate, visible signal that something's wrong, which makes gut health products some of the easiest items to sell with confidence.

The science backing it up has also matured fast. Researchers now talk about a "microbiome-immune axis" — the idea that a huge share of seemingly unrelated problems (itchy skin, anxiety, poor coat quality, weight issues) actually trace back to gut bacteria balance. That reframing turns a niche supplement aisle into the foundation of your entire wellness category.

Know Your Three Letters: Pro-, Pre-, and Postbiotics

You don't need a microbiology degree, but you do need to explain this correctly to customers, because the three terms are not interchangeable:

  • Probiotics — live beneficial bacteria (think Purina FortiFlora, Zesty Paws). The category pet owners already recognize.
  • Prebiotics — fibers like chicory root and inulin that feed the good bacteria already living in the gut. Often baked directly into kibble now.
  • Postbiotics — the byproducts of fermentation (not live organisms). This is the fastest-growing of the three because postbiotics survive extrusion, baking, and shelf storage far better than live probiotic strains, which makes them cheaper and easier for manufacturers to put into dry food without losing potency.

Brands like Fera Pets, ADM Protexin, and PetCultures are leaning hard into postbiotic formulas for everything from digestion to dental health to anxiety. IFF's new PureStrong probiotic, sourced from the microbiome of healthy dogs, is a good example of the R&D money pouring into this space.

The Format Question: Chews Win, And It's Not Close

If you're deciding what to stock, format matters as much as ingredient. Chewable and soft-chew formats now capture 48% of the entire pet dietary supplements market — pet owners are tired of fighting their dogs and cats to swallow a pill, and a chew that looks and smells like a treat removes that friction entirely. Stock chews front and center; treat capsules and powders as a secondary option for picky eaters or pets on multiple medications where a vet has asked for tighter dosing control.

The Numbers That Justify the Shelf Space

This isn't a hunch — it's one of the best-documented growth categories in pet retail right now:

  • The pet probiotics supplement market is projected to grow from $1.45 billion in 2026 to $2.61 billion by 2031 — an 11.4% CAGR.
  • Digestive health is the single largest use case inside that market, at 41.8% of total sales.
  • 56% of dog owners and 54% of cat owners actively look for "digestive health" claims when scanning a label or product page.
  • Postbiotic-specific products are growing even faster — a 150% five-year annual growth rate in new product launches, well ahead of probiotics or prebiotics individually.

Translate that into merchandising terms: gut health is no longer a side category you stock because a few customers ask for it. It's becoming a baseline expectation, the same way "grain-free" was a decade ago.

How to Merchandise It Without Confusing Your Customers

The biggest risk in this category isn't lack of demand — it's customer confusion. Most pet owners can't tell a probiotic from a postbiotic, and a wall of unfamiliar science terms will make them bounce to a product they already recognize. Three fixes that work:

  • Lead with the symptom, not the ingredient. Category headers like "Upset Stomach & Loose Stool," "Skin & Coat Support," and "Calm & Anxiety" convert far better than "Probiotics" as a standalone aisle label.
  • Bundle by life stage and condition. A "senior dog gut + joint" bundle or a "puppy digestive starter kit" gives customers a decision-made-for-them path instead of forcing them to compare ingredient panels.
  • Use one sentence of plain-English education per product page. "Postbiotics are the calming, gut-friendly byproducts left behind after good bacteria do their job — shelf-stable and easy to digest" does more conversion work than a clinical ingredient list.

The Margin Story Is Excellent

Gut health supplements sit in the same 40-60% gross margin range as the broader pet supplement category, but they have one advantage most supplements don't: extremely high repeat-purchase behavior. A joint supplement might get reordered every 60-90 days; a daily probiotic or postbiotic chew gets consumed on a strict daily schedule, which makes it one of the cleanest products in your entire catalog to put on autoship. If a customer buys a 30-count gut health chew, you already know the exact day they'll run out.

Where Chewy and Amazon Are Still Weak Here

Chewy recently acquired Modern Animal, signaling how seriously the big players are taking the broader pet health ecosystem — but their product pages are still built for search-and-buy, not education. There's no one explaining the difference between a probiotic and a postbiotic, no symptom-led navigation, no "ask an expert" moment. That's exactly the gap an independent store can fill: a short quiz ("itchy skin," "soft stool," "seems anxious") that routes customers to the right 2-3 product options beats a 200-SKU wall of unsorted supplements every time.

Start Small, Then Build the Whole Aisle

You don't need 40 SKUs to capture this trend. Pick 4-5 well-reviewed products across the three categories — a probiotic chew, a prebiotic-fortified topper, and a postbiotic formula for a specific concern like anxiety or skin — and build symptom-led merchandising around them. The data says this category keeps growing through 2031 at minimum. Stores that build the expertise now will own the customer relationships before the big platforms figure out how to sell nuance at scale.

Gut health products are some of the easiest autoship conversions you'll ever make.

Daily chews and probiotic toppers run out on a predictable schedule — set your customers up to never run out, and save them 15% along the way.
