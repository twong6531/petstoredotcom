The Anxiety Economy: Why Calming Products Are Quietly Becoming a Top Pet Store Category

📅 June 22, 2026 ✍️ PetStore.com Editorial Team 🏷️ Wellness · Merchandising · Pet Behavior

44% of pet owners say they actively worry about their pet's separation anxiety. Not "have noticed it once" — worry about it, the way you'd worry about a kid's sleep schedule. That single stat, from a 2026 survey of 2,000 pet owners, explains why the global pet calming products market is on track to roughly double from $1.2 billion in 2025 to $2.06 billion by 2030. If your store still treats "calming" as a single shelf of chews near the checkout counter, you're underselling one of the fastest-growing, highest-margin categories in pet retail.

The Anxiety Economy Is Bigger Than You Think

Depending on which research firm you ask, the broader "pet calming products" market — which includes pheromones, calming wearables, supplements, and noise/anxiety-specific devices — is valued anywhere from $3.8 billion to $18.47 billion globally in 2026, growing at 6-11.5% CAGR. The range is wide because definitions vary, but the direction is unanimous: this is not a niche. Chewable calming supplements alone captured 34.7% of the category in 2025, and North America accounts for 41.2% of global demand.

The "why" is straightforward. Pet humanization keeps accelerating, anxiety awareness in humans has bled directly into how owners interpret their pets' behavior, and post-pandemic separation issues never fully went away — a lot of dogs got used to owners being home 24/7 and never adjusted when offices reopened.

Your Customers Are Already Anxious — About Their Pet's Anxiety

Prevalence estimates for canine separation anxiety vary wildly by methodology — anywhere from 9.3% (strict clinical criteria) to 20-40% (looser self-reported behavior). For your purposes as a retailer, the exact number matters less than the psychology: nearly half of pet owners are anxious enough about the possibility that they'll buy a product to address it preemptively, not just reactively.

That's a fundamentally different sales motion than most categories. You're not selling a cure for a diagnosed problem — you're selling peace of mind to an owner who feels guilty leaving for work. Lead your content and product copy with empathy for that guilt, not clinical jargon.

The Core Product Stack to Carry

You don't need 200 SKUs. You need a tight, credible lineup across price points and use cases:

  • Pheromone systems — ThunderEase (powered by the Adaptil formula) is the category leader, with over 80% of surveyed pet parents reporting improvement. Stock the diffuser kit, collar, and travel spray — three price points, three occasions.
  • Pressure wraps — ThunderShirt's classic wrap ($45) remains the go-to for acute panic situations like thunderstorms and fireworks.
  • Calming chews — the single biggest sub-segment by revenue share. Look for formulas built on L-theanine, melatonin, and tryptophan; chewable formats dominate specifically because owners hate fighting their pet to swallow a pill.
  • Lick mats and slow feeders — a genuinely under-stocked behavioral category. The repetitive licking motion triggers a calming response, and at a $2-5 cost to source against a $14-22 retail price, the margins are absurd.
  • Cooling mats — not strictly "calming," but they solve the same emotional job (reducing pet distress) and pair naturally with anxiety bundles for hot climates or travel.

A combo of a ThunderShirt ($45) plus an Adaptil-style diffuser ($25) handles roughly 80% of everyday anxiety cases for under $70 — that's a real, sellable bundle, not marketing fluff.

The Margins Are Genuinely Excellent

This is the part that should get your attention as a buyer, not just a behaviorist. Anxiety-relief products built around TikTok/Instagram demand routinely cost $2-5 to source and sell for $14-22 — margins north of 60%. Veterinarian-formulated supplements and clinically backed pheromone products command real price premiums because the credibility is built in. Compare that to the 40-65% you'll see on general pet accessories, and calming is consistently at the top of the range.

Build a "First Vet Visit Survival Kit" or "New Puppy Calm-Down Bundle" combining a chew, a lick mat, and a pheromone spray. Bundling against an emotional trigger (a vet visit, a move, a new baby in the house) converts dramatically better than selling the same three SKUs separately.

Sell With Trust, Not Hype

This category has a credibility problem — Amazon and TikTok Shop are flooded with unbranded calming chews making vague claims. You win by being the opposite of that. Cite the actual mechanism (L-theanine, pheromone mimicry, pressure therapy) in your product descriptions instead of just "calms your pet." Link to real behaviorist content. If a product has vet-recommended status or third-party testing, say so prominently — "#1 Vet Recommended" is doing real work for ThunderEase's positioning, and you should borrow that credibility signal pattern in your own copy.

Short video content performs exceptionally well here: a 30-second clip of a dog visibly relaxing after a ThunderShirt goes on, or destroying a lick mat in blissful silence, is the kind of content that gets shared by owners who feel seen. Anxiety-relief content taps directly into the guilt-driven purchase psychology that makes pet owners spend instinctively rather than logically.

Where to Place It (Both Online and On-Site)

Don't bury this category under "Accessories" or "Health." Give it its own collection page — "Calm & Comfort" or similar — and cross-merchandise aggressively: link calming chews from every new-puppy and senior-pet product page, surface a cooling mat on every "summer travel" landing page, and trigger a post-purchase email offering a calming bundle to anyone who just bought a crate, carrier, or boarding-related product. Those are the exact moments an owner's anxiety about their pet's anxiety peaks.

The Bottom Line

The calming products category rewards stores that treat it as a wellness conversation, not a clearance-bin afterthought. The math is on your side: 60%+ margins, a market growing faster than almost any other pet segment, and a customer base that is emotionally primed to buy. Curate three to five credible products, bundle them around real-life triggers, and lead with empathy in your copy.

And once a customer finds the calming chew or lick mat that actually works for their dog, don't make them remember to reorder it — that's a guaranteed repeat purchase sitting in plain sight.
