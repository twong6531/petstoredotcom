Chewy's Autoship Drives 82% of Their Revenue. Here's How to Build the Same Loyalty Engine for Your Pet Store.

📅 June 14, 2026 ✍️ PetStore.com Editorial Team 🏷️ Loyalty · Email Marketing · Retention

Chewy doesn't win because of their prices — their prices are competitive, not remarkable. They win because 82.2% of their net sales come from Autoship subscribers, a customer base that has essentially pre-committed to buying from them every month. That's not a marketing channel. That's a loyalty engine. The good news for independent pet stores: the same psychological and economic forces that make Chewy's Autoship work are completely replicable at your scale — often with better emotional depth than a $3.1 billion corporation can manufacture. Here's exactly how to build it.

Why Pet Stores Are the Perfect Business for Loyalty Programs

Most retail categories have a retention problem. Pet stores have the opposite: they have a natural retention advantage that most owners squander by failing to capitalize on it.

Think about what your customers actually buy: pet food, treats, supplements, litter. These are consumables with predictable replenishment cycles — a bag of food lasts 3-4 weeks, a bag of litter 2-3 weeks, a supplement bottle 30-60 days. Your customers will buy again. The question is whether they come back to you or drift to whoever pings them first with a coupon code.

The data backs this up hard. Pet supply brands already exceed 30% repeat customer rates — well above the general ecommerce average. And companies with structured loyalty programs report an average 235% increase in repeat customer rate after implementation. If you're selling consumables and you don't have a loyalty program, you're essentially running an inefficient repeat-purchase business with zero infrastructure to protect it.

The Three Loyalty Mechanics: Which One Fits Your Store

Not all loyalty programs are the same. There are three primary mechanics, and each has a different fit depending on your order volume, average ticket size, and customer base.

Points programs work best for high-frequency, lower-ticket purchases. Customers earn points per dollar spent and redeem them for discounts or free products. Simple, universally understood, easy to launch with Smile.io (which has a free starter tier for Shopify stores). The downside: points programs can train customers to hold purchases until they're redeeming, which can create revenue lumpiness.

Tiered programs are where the real ROI lives. The data is unambiguous: tiered loyalty programs achieve 1.8x higher ROI than non-tiered structures. VIP customers in tier-based programs generate 73% higher average order value ($435 vs. $291) and purchase 3.6x more frequently than standard customers. A three-tier structure — Bronze, Silver, Gold — with meaningful perks at each level (early access to new products, free samples, dedicated email support) creates aspirational spending behavior that flat points programs simply can't match.

Punch card programs are the underrated dark horse. Doodle Dogs, an independent pet boutique, runs a simple punch card program — and loyalty members have a 1.6x higher average order value, shop 4.6x more frequently, and generate 25% of the company's total revenue. Low-tech, high-output. Don't dismiss it.

The Email Flows That Get 40–55% Open Rates

Standard marketing emails in ecommerce average around 20% open rates on a good day. Loyalty-triggered emails — points balance updates, tier advancement notifications, exclusive member offers — consistently see 40–55% open rates. That's not a small difference. That's a different category of marketing.

The flows that drive this are all variants of the same idea: give your loyalty members information that's specifically relevant to them, at the moment it's relevant. The highest-performing sequences:

  • Points milestone alerts — "You're 200 points from your next reward" — drive immediate purchase behavior. These should trigger automatically in Klaviyo or Omnisend when a customer crosses a threshold.
  • Tier upgrade notifications — celebrating when a customer hits Silver or Gold status is one of the few moments in retail where a customer opens a congratulations email and feels genuinely good about it.
  • Expiring rewards — 30-day, 14-day, and 7-day countdown emails for expiring points consistently generate some of the highest single-email revenue you'll see.
  • Win-back with loyalty hook — if a loyalty member goes 60+ days without purchasing, a "your points are waiting for you" email outperforms a generic discount offer every time.

The unifying principle: personalized emails generate 6x higher transaction rates than generic campaigns. Your loyalty data is your personalization engine — use it.

Segment by Pet Type, Not Just Purchase History

Most pet stores segment by what a customer bought last. Smart ones segment by who their customer's pet is.

Ask the question on signup: "Do you have a dog, cat, bird, reptile, or small mammal?" Then layer in pet size and age. A Golden Retriever owner and a Chihuahua owner have completely different product needs, purchase cycles, and price sensitivities — sending them the same email is leaving conversion on the table.

Platforms like Klaviyo support custom profile properties that let you tag every customer with their pet type and trigger segment-specific flows. A cat owner who bought litter six weeks ago should get a reorder reminder at week 5 — not a promotion for dog harnesses. This sounds obvious, but fewer than 20% of independent pet stores actually do it systematically.

Secondary segmentation by age matters too. Senior pet owners spend significantly more on supplements, joint support, and prescription-adjacent wellness products. Puppy and kitten owners are buying in a burst — they need starter kits and onboarding sequences that introduce them to your full catalog. These are different customers with different needs, and your email program should treat them that way.

Birthday Campaigns: The Easiest Revenue You're Not Capturing

Birthday campaigns consistently outperform every other retail email in open rates and redemption rates. The difference in pet is even more pronounced: you're not celebrating the customer's birthday, you're celebrating their pet's birthday — and the emotional connection pet owners have with that date is intense.

A simple birthday flow looks like this: collect the pet's birthday at signup (or via a post-purchase survey), trigger an email two weeks before that date with a modest loyalty bonus or percentage-off coupon, and follow up on the day itself with a "Happy Birthday [Pet's Name]!" message featuring birthday-appropriate products (birthday cake-flavored treats, personalized bandanas, toy bundles).

The numbers on birthday campaigns in pet ecommerce are consistently among the highest open and redemption rates of any email program — often 3-4x the open rate of standard promotional sends. And importantly, these customers are already planning a celebration. Your email is meeting them where they already are, not asking them to generate a new purchase impulse.

Stack Loyalty With Autoship for Maximum Lifetime Value

The real unlock is combining your loyalty program with an autoship offering. Each one works well independently; together they create a compounding retention engine.

Here's how to structure it: make autoship enrollment a loyalty program action that awards a significant one-time points bonus (enough to be genuinely exciting — a free product or a meaningful discount on their next order). Then award bonus points on every autoship fulfillment, making the autoship tier feel more valuable than single-purchase ordering.

BarkBox found that subscribers who added a charitable giving option to their subscription had 10–20% higher retention than standard subscribers. The principle is the same: layering meaning and value onto a subscription deepens the relationship in ways that price matching cannot touch. Exclusive autoship-member content, early access to new products, and "subscriber only" product lines all serve the same function.

Currently, 48% of U.S. pet owners already use subscription services for pet products. These customers have already decided they want this model. You're not convincing them to try something new — you're competing for their subscription loyalty. The store that bundles a compelling loyalty program with its autoship offering wins that competition without needing to be the lowest price in the room.

The Tools to Launch This in 30 Days

The barrier to entry here is lower than most store owners think. For a Shopify store doing $10K–$100K/month in revenue, the right stack is:

  • Smile.io — Free starter tier, points and referral programs, native Shopify integration. Launch in a weekend. Upgrade to Growth ($49/mo) once you're generating meaningful loyalty revenue.
  • Yotpo Loyalty — Better analytics and AI-powered personalization, plus native integration with Yotpo Reviews. Good choice if you want loyalty and social proof in the same platform. Mid-market pricing starts around $199/mo.
  • LoyaltyLion — The loyalty specialist. Deeper customization for tiered programs, better for stores with high AOV ($150+) and complex customer journeys. Starts at $199/mo, worth it at higher volumes.
  • Klaviyo or Omnisend — Either works for email automation. Connect to your loyalty platform so points milestones, tier changes, and birthday triggers fire automatically. No manual work after setup.

Week 1: Install Smile.io, set up your points rules, create a welcome bonus for new signups. Week 2: Connect Klaviyo and build your milestone and win-back flows. Week 3: Launch a pet birthday collection campaign to your existing list to seed birthday data. Week 4: Audit your autoship offer and add a loyalty enrollment bonus. That's a complete retention engine built in a month.

The ROI benchmark to hold yourself to: loyalty program members should generate 12–18% more incremental revenue per year than non-members. If you're not hitting that after 90 days, your points values or email triggers need adjustment — not the strategy itself.

The Bottom Line

Chewy has built an $11 billion annual revenue business on one core insight: if you give pet owners a simple, convenient reason to keep buying from you, they will. Their Autoship program is not sophisticated. It's just built, maintained, and persistently promoted.

Your independent pet store has one structural advantage Chewy will never have: the ability to make customers feel like they're buying from someone who genuinely cares about their pet, not a fulfillment algorithm. Combine that emotional advantage with a properly built loyalty program and automated email flows, and you've created a retention engine that compounds every month.

Members who redeem loyalty rewards spend 3.1x more annually than members who don't. That number is sitting in your existing customer base right now, waiting for you to build the infrastructure to capture it.
