78% of Pet Owners Travel With Their Dogs This Summer — Here's the $8.4 Billion Travel Accessories Category Your Store Is Barely Touching

📅 July 12, 2026 ✍️ PetStore.com Editorial Team 🏷️ Strategy · Travel · Seasonal Revenue

Here's a number that should make you stop scrolling: 78% of American pet owners now travel with their pets every year. That's not a niche behavior — it's the majority of your customer base, right now, in the middle of peak travel season, looking for products you may not be stocking. The pet travel accessories market hits $8.42 billion in 2026 and is growing at 6.6% CAGR toward $12.36 billion by 2033. Most independent pet stores treat this category as a few leashes and a collapsible bowl near the checkout. That gap between where the market is and where your shelf is? That's your revenue opportunity for the rest of the summer.

The Pet Travel Boom That's Happening Right Now

Summer 2026 is officially the biggest pet travel season on record. The share of pet owners who choose to bring their animals along when they travel has surged from 17% in 2024 to 26% in 2026 — a 53% jump in two years. As of June 2026, 63% of pet owners planned to travel within the next three months, up from 61% the prior year. That's tens of millions of pets hitting the road.

The "why" isn't complicated: post-pandemic pet ownership fundamentally changed the relationship between Americans and their animals. Pets became family members in a way that made leaving them behind feel genuinely wrong. At the same time, the hospitality industry adapted — around 75% of hotels across luxury, mid-scale, and economy categories now allow pets. The infrastructure for pet travel finally caught up with the emotional reality of pet ownership.

The result is a massive, underserved demand spike that hits hardest in June, July, and August — and most pet stores aren't ready for it.

Why Pet Stores Are Leaving This Category on the Table

Walk into the average independent pet store — physical or online — and the "travel" section is an afterthought. A few collapsible bowls. A spare leash hook. Maybe a carrier that's been sitting there since 2023.

The problem is category thinking. Travel accessories get mentally filed under "accessories" — low-priority, low-margin, low-turns. That framing is wrong. Pet travel gear is:

  • High-margin — accessories consistently deliver 40–65% gross margins, compared to 25–35% on food
  • High-urgency — customers buying for an upcoming trip have a deadline, which kills price sensitivity
  • Emotionally loaded — no one skimps on their dog's safety in the car. That's a premium-converting dynamic.
  • Upsell-rich — a customer who buys a carrier also needs a travel water bottle, calming chews, an ID tag update, and a portable food mat

The car travel segment alone is projected to account for 28.3% of the pet travel accessories market in 2026, driven by the simple fact that 64% of pet owners prefer road trips with their pets over any other travel method. You don't need to build a massive travel category from scratch — you need to own the car travel aisle.

The Six Products Your Travel Section Needs Right Now

You don't need 50 SKUs. You need the right ones. Here's what to prioritize based on consumer data and search volume right now:

1. Crash-tested car harnesses ($35–$90). The Kurgo Tru-Fit Smart Harness is crash-tested, features five adjustable points and quick-release buckles, and includes a 10-inch seatbelt tether. It's one of the most-searched pet travel products on Amazon in June–August, every year. Carry it. Know it. Recommend it confidently.

2. Convertible carriers ($80–$200). The Ibiyaya Eva Convertible functions as a stroller, backpack, shoulder carrier, and car seat for small dogs and cats up to 17.6 lbs. Airline-approved carriers that do double duty are consistently the highest-conversion items in the category — pet owners flying with their animals have no choice but to buy something, and they buy quality.

3. Car seat barriers and back-seat hammocks ($30–$65). High-volume, low-cost, impulse-friendly. Protect the car, protect the dog, easy installation. This is your volume driver — the item that goes in the cart alongside everything else.

4. GPS tracking collars ($60–$180). Travel creates a very real fear of losing a pet. FitBark, Tractive, and Whistle all offer GPS collars with app connectivity and geofencing alerts. The Tractive GPS Dog LTE sells over 1 million units annually. This fear-driven category converts exceptionally well around travel season and provides a real recurring subscription model through monthly tracking fees.

5. Travel water bottles and collapsible kits ($15–$45). These are your add-to-cart champions — low price, high utility, easy gifting. Bundle them into every travel purchase. A "road trip starter pack" — harness, collapsible bowl, portable water bottle — sells for $75–$110 and is three separate items that cost you $35–$55 to assemble.

6. Portable travel beds and mats ($40–$120). K&H Pet Products and Ruffwear both make packable, washable options that dog owners camping or staying in hotels genuinely need. Ruffwear's Highlands Pad and Camp Bed sell particularly well May through September. Stock the mid-tier at $50–$80 — that's where conversion is highest.

Car Travel Is Your Biggest Opportunity — Stock Accordingly

Don't overthink the channel split: 64% of pet owners prefer car travel with their pets. That's your primary market. Build your travel category around the car experience, and the rest of the category supports it naturally.

The safety angle is critical here. Unrestrained dogs in cars are a genuine danger — studies show that a 50-pound dog becomes a 1,500-pound projectile in a 30 mph crash. That's not a statistic you lead with to scare customers, but it's the reason why crash-tested harnesses from brands like Kurgo, Sleepypod, and Ruffwear command premium prices and why customers who understand the risk are willing to pay.

Build a "car safety bundle" — a crash-tested harness plus a back-seat hammock plus a portable water bottle — priced at $99–$135. Name it something concrete: "The Road Trip Essentials Kit." Customers searching for travel gear respond to bundles because they remove decision fatigue. You've already done the curating work; they just say yes.

The Safety Angle That Converts Like Nothing Else

Here's the fundamental emotional engine behind pet travel purchases: guilt and fear. Not in a manipulative way — in a very human way. Pet owners who travel with their animals are anxious about their pets' safety, comfort, and wellbeing in unfamiliar environments. That anxiety drives purchase decisions far more reliably than aspirational lifestyle marketing.

Your product content and email marketing should lean into this directly. "Is your car setup actually safe for your dog?" is a more compelling subject line than "New Travel Gear Available." "What to do if your dog bolts at a rest stop" is a more clicked blog post than "Top 5 Travel Tips." Content that acknowledges the real fear — and then resolves it with specific products — is the highest-converting format in this category.

Pair safety messaging with expert positioning. You're not just stocking a harness — you're the store that knows which harness actually passed crash testing and why the $19 Amazon version doesn't protect your dog. That expertise is what keeps customers from just buying the cheapest option and hoping for the best.

How to Merchandise and Bundle for Maximum AOV

Pet travel accessories are unusually bundle-friendly because the use case is clear, the items are complementary, and the purchase occasion has a fixed timeline. Somebody leaving for a road trip next Friday needs everything at once. Take advantage of that.

Three bundle strategies that work:

The Road Trip Bundle ($99–$125): Crash-tested harness + back-seat hammock + collapsible travel bowl + portable water bottle. Name it, price it together, photograph it styled in or next to a car. This is your volume seller.

The Anxious Traveler Bundle ($85–$110): GPS collar or tracker + ID tag (with your store's phone number as an emergency contact — good community marketing) + calming chews + comfort travel mat. This targets owners traveling for the first time with their pet, or owners whose dogs are anxious about unfamiliar environments.

The Weekend Adventurer Bundle ($120–$160): Ruffwear adventure harness + Ruffwear Camp Bed or K&H outdoor bed + portable food/water kit + poop bag dispenser. Target the hiking and camping segment — it skews younger and spends more.

Promote these bundles in three places: a dedicated travel landing page, a post-purchase email to anyone who's bought food in the last 60 days ("Are you taking your dog on vacation this summer?"), and your social media content for the next six weeks. It's peak season. Act like it.

Turning Summer Shoppers Into Year-Round Regulars

Here's the challenge with seasonal categories: you get the spike, and then what? The answer is to treat every travel-occasion purchase as the start of a relationship, not a one-time transaction.

The GPS collar subscriber is already on a monthly plan — your job is to make sure that recurring relationship is with you. Build post-purchase flows that check in at 30, 60, and 90 days. "How was the trip? Your dog's calming chews are probably running low — here's a 15% discount on your next order." That's the autoship onboarding sequence for a seasonal buyer.

Travel buyers are also food buyers, supplement buyers, and enrichment buyers. The average pet owner spends $1,200+ annually on their animals. Someone who found your store because you had the best car harness recommendation on the internet can become a $100/month autoship subscriber if you handle the post-purchase experience correctly.

The travel category isn't just a summer revenue bump — it's a customer acquisition channel that happens to hit when people are in a buying mindset, spending freely, and urgently in need of expert guidance. That combination doesn't come around often. Stock for it now.

The Bottom Line

The $8.4 billion pet travel accessories market is growing at 6.6% annually and peak season is happening right now. Your customers are planning road trips, camping weekends, and family vacations — and a majority of them are bringing their dogs. The category delivers 40–65% margins, bundles naturally, and converts at premium price points because it's safety-driven, not bargain-driven.

You don't need to overhaul your inventory. You need to stock the six core products, build two or three smart bundles, and write the content that positions you as the expert who actually knows the difference between a crash-tested harness and a novelty item. That's enough to own the category before your competitors figure out it exists.
