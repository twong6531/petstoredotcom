58% of Pet Owners Think Your Labels Are Lying — Why Radical Transparency Is Your New Competitive Edge

📅 June 30, 2026 ✍️ PetStore.com Editorial Team 🏷️ Trust · Transparency · Pet Industry

Here's a number that should worry every pet retailer: 58% of pet owners believe pet food labels are misleading, according to a PETS International / Yummypets survey of 800+ owners across the US, Canada, the UK, and France. Four in ten find words like "natural" and "premium" confusing or dishonest. Nearly a third don't trust the nutritional claims, and a fifth don't even trust the photos on the bag. Your customers have stopped believing the marketing — and the store that proves its claims instead of just making them is about to become the only one they trust.

The Trust Gap: Vague Claims Aren't Working Anymore

Pet owners aren't tuning out information — they're starving for it. 90% of US dog and cat owners say it's important to read ingredient labels before buying, and 86% are more likely to purchase when they recognize most of the ingredients listed. Yet 6 out of 10 still say accurate, transparent labeling is "very important" while simultaneously believing brands aren't delivering it. That gap — high scrutiny, low trust — is exactly where independent stores can win. Chewy and Amazon sell SKUs. They don't have the bandwidth or incentive to vouch for every ingredient on every product page. You do.

Stop Selling Claims. Start Selling Proof.

"Premium," "human-grade," and "natural" are unregulated marketing words — and pet owners know it now. What they're asking for instead is documentation. For supplements especially, a Certificate of Analysis (COA) from an accredited lab is becoming the baseline expectation, not a nice-to-have. A label is a promise; a COA is proof — it shows exactly what was tested, how, and what the lot actually contained.

Brands like Lillie&Lee have started publishing third-party COAs across their entire line, verifying potency and screening for contaminants on every batch. When you stock a supplement brand, ask the rep one question before you commit shelf space: "Can I link a COA on the product page?" If the answer is no, that's a signal — and increasingly, a dealbreaker for your most loyal customers.

Make Sourcing Visible, Not Buried in Fine Print

93% of pet owners now believe companies should be more open about their supply chain — 77% want to know the ingredients, 73% want to know where those ingredients actually came from. 73% of dog owners and 70% of cat owners specifically want brands to make ingredient origin easier to track.

QR codes are how that's happening at scale. QR scans on pet food packaging hit 41.77 million in 2025, up 44% year-over-year, and 60% of shoppers now scan a code before buying. Open Farm's "Sourced & Certified" program is the model worth studying: a traceability code on every bag lets a customer look up the exact farm, harvest date, and even the boat that brought the fish in. One premium nutrition brand saw a 17% higher likelihood of purchase simply from linking packaging QR codes to sourcing and certification data. If a supplier offers that kind of traceability, put it front and center on your product page — don't make the customer dig for it.

Write Product Pages Like a Vet, Not a Copywriter

Replace adjective-heavy copy with specifics. Instead of "premium, natural ingredients," write "deboned chicken (38%), wild-caught salmon oil (omega-3 source), no corn, wheat, or soy — full sourcing breakdown linked below." Instead of "supports joint health," write "1,000mg glucosamine and 800mg chondroitin per serving, third-party tested, COA linked."

This is slower to write than generic marketing copy, but it's exactly the long-tail, expert content that builds search authority and converts skeptical buyers. A customer who reads a transparent, specific product page once will check your site first the next time — because you've already proven you don't hide behind buzzwords.

Audit Your Existing Brand Lineup This Week

You don't need to overhaul your whole catalog overnight. Pull your top 20 best-sellers and run each one through three questions:

  • Can this brand produce a COA or lab test on request? If yes, get it linked on the page.
  • Does this brand disclose ingredient origin? If yes, surface it. If no, ask why — and consider whether a more transparent competitor deserves the shelf space instead.
  • Are the claims on this product specific or vague? Rewrite vague ones using the brand's own spec sheets, not their ad copy.

Brands that already do this work for you — increasingly the norm in supplements and raw/freeze-dried categories — should get priority placement. You're not just selling their product; you're lending them your store's credibility. Make sure they've earned it.

Turn Transparency Into Merchandising, Not Just Policy

Don't bury this work in an "About Us" page nobody reads. Create a visible trust signal at the category and product level: a small badge — "Lab Verified," "Sourcing Disclosed," "COA Available" — next to qualifying products, with a one-click link to the actual document. Feature a rotating "Transparency Spotlight" on your homepage or in your newsletter, profiling one brand's sourcing story per month. This is content that's nearly impossible for Chewy or Amazon to replicate at scale, because it requires a curator who actually knows and vets every brand on the shelf — which is the entire value proposition of an independent store.

Why This Beats Chewy and Amazon at Their Own Game

Marketplaces compete on price, speed, and selection. They cannot credibly compete on "we personally vetted every ingredient in this bag and can prove it." That's a trust relationship, not a logistics one — and trust is exactly what 58% of pet owners say they're missing right now. Build a reputation as the store that shows its work, and price stops being the deciding factor. Customers pay a premium for certainty.

The Bottom Line

Your customers aren't reading less — they're reading more carefully, and they don't believe what they find. The pet stores that win the next few years won't be the ones with the slickest ad copy. They'll be the ones who replace "natural" and "premium" with COAs, sourcing maps, and specific numbers — and make that proof impossible to miss.

Start with your top 20 SKUs this week. The trust you build there compounds into every repeat order after it.
