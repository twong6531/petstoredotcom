National Pet Brands Are Losing Ground. Private Label Is Winning. Here's the 2026 Playbook for Launching Your Own Line.

📅 July 10, 2026 ✍️ PetStore.com Editorial Team 🏷️ Strategy · Private Label · Margins

Here's a number that should make you put down your coffee: national pet brand unit sales declined 0.6% in 2024. Private label pet care unit sales, over the same period, grew 3.5%. And while you were stocking Hill's Science Diet at 20% gross margin, a pet store in your market was selling the exact same functional hip-and-joint soft chew formula under their own label at 35% margin. The private label revolution in pet products is not coming. It's here. The question is whether your store is going to ride it or watch it from the sidelines.

Why Private Label Is Winning Right Now

The global pet food OEM and private label market sits at $54.45 billion in 2026 and is projected to reach $126.45 billion by 2035 — a 10% CAGR that is quietly outpacing almost every other segment of the pet industry. Three forces are converging at exactly the right moment for indie store owners.

First, consumer trust in store brands has hit an all-time high. Inflation conditioned pet owners to look at alternatives to name brands, and what they found was that private label quality had caught up dramatically. Today's retailer-owned formulas carry grain-free, high-protein, novel protein, and functional health claims identical to the premium nationals. Once shoppers try a store brand and it works, they stick with it — and they trust the retailer who vouched for it.

Second, your margins on nationals are structurally compressed. National brands spend heavily on advertising, retail promotions, and slotting fees. Those costs come out of your margin. Private label cuts out the middleman: your production cost is your cost of goods, period. That's why private label consistently delivers 8–12 percentage points more gross margin than equivalent national brand products in the same category.

Third, pet owners increasingly buy the store, not the brand. If your customers trust you enough to take your recommendation on raw food, they'll trust a supplement with your name on the label. Your credibility IS the brand. A private label line is just a way to monetize that trust directly.

Start With Supplements and Treats, Not Kibble

The most common mistake first-time private label operators make: going straight for kibble or wet food. Don't. Dry extruded kibble has minimum order quantities (MOQs) starting at 20,000–40,000 pounds. Wet canned food demands 40,000–80,000 cans per run. That's a six-figure commitment before you've sold a single unit and before you know if your customers will even buy your house brand.

Supplements and soft-chew treats are the correct entry point. MOQs are a fraction of food products, quality co-packers are abundant, margins are strong, and the regulatory requirements are lower than for complete pet food.

  • Soft chews — MOQs start at 504–800 units at most supplement co-packers. A run of 500 hip-and-joint soft chews with your label and packaging can come in under $5,000 total investment.
  • Functional treats — baked or freeze-dried treats often have 200–500 unit minimums with white-label treat specialists. Low risk, high visibility on shelf.
  • Powder supplements — digestive enzymes, omega blends, and greens powders are straightforward to private label and have strong consumer demand. MOQs vary by manufacturer but 100–200 unit runs are achievable.

Start with one product. Learn the process. Then expand. This is not a category you want to over-invest in before you've validated demand with your specific customer base.

Finding Your Co-Packer: Names, Numbers, and What to Ask

A co-packer (contract manufacturer) makes the product to your specification and puts your label on it. Here's a short list worth a first call:

  • Pet Tech Labs (pettechlabs.com) — soft chews, capsules, powders. MOQs from 800 units. NASC-member facility with a streamlined private label process. Good starting point for supplements.
  • Cassco Bio Labs (cassco-bio.com) — functional chews and soft chews, pet specialty. Transparent about their process and compliance requirements.
  • Uckele Health & Nutrition (uckele.com) — premium private label formulations in powders and pellets, human and animal nutrition. Higher quality bar, priced accordingly.
  • PetDine LLC (petdinellc.com) — white-label treats and chews, focused specifically on pet retail. Good for treat-first launches.
  • Gold Line Pet Foods (goldline-petfoods.com) — if you eventually want to expand into food, they do private label kibble and treats with more accessible guidance for smaller first runs.
  • Nutribl (nutribl.com) — UK-based but ships to US. Offers no-minimum-order-quantity pet nutrition private label — genuinely useful for testing a formula before committing to a full run.

Questions to ask every co-packer on your first call:

  • What are your standard MOQs, and what's the price break at 2x volume?
  • Do you carry your own product liability insurance?
  • Can I see your most recent facility audit certificate (SQF, BRC, or GFSI equivalent)?
  • What's your typical lead time from order to delivery?
  • Do you offer stock formulas, and do you handle AAFCO label review or is that my responsibility?

That last question matters more than most store owners realize. Stock formulas — proven formulations the co-packer already makes for multiple clients — slash your time to market from 6+ months to 60–90 days and eliminate the $15,000+ cost of custom formulation. Start with a stock formula. Differentiate through your branding, not your ingredient list.

The Compliance Checklist (Don't Skip This)

Pet food and supplements operate under FDA oversight, and AAFCO model regulations govern state-level labeling requirements. This sounds scarier than it is — your co-packer handles the manufacturing compliance — but you're responsible for what's on your label. Every pet product label must include:

  • Product name and the species it's intended for
  • Net quantity statement
  • Guaranteed analysis (protein, fat, fiber, moisture percentages for food; key actives for supplements)
  • Ingredient list in descending order by weight
  • Nutritional adequacy statement (for complete pet foods only, not supplements)
  • Feeding or dosing directions
  • Manufacturer or distributor name and address (yours, or the co-packer's with your name)

For supplements specifically, NASC (National Animal Supplement Council) membership and their Quality Seal cost roughly $1,200/year and add meaningful consumer trust. Your co-packer should already be NASC-compliant if they're serious about pet supplements — confirm this upfront. For your first product, lean on the co-packer's compliance infrastructure. As you scale, consider engaging an AAFCO-specialist regulatory consultant (expect $150–$250/hour, 3–5 hours for a first product review).

Packaging That Looks Like a $40 Premium Product

Pet owners judge a supplement by its bag before they read the ingredient panel. The good news: premium packaging is cheaper than at any point in history.

The format: flat-bottom pouches with matte finish and spot UV gloss on your logo. These look like what's sitting on the shelf at a specialty pet retailer. Services like Sticker Mule, noissue, and Berlin Packaging offer custom stand-up pouches starting at $1.50–$2.50 per unit in runs of 500+. For 500 units at $2 each, your packaging cost is $1,000 — a rounding error compared to the margin you'll capture.

The branding: clean, purposeful, and species-specific. Avoid the temptation to make one generic design that covers "all pets." A hip-and-joint chew for dogs should look like it was designed for dogs. A bag featuring an action shot of a golden retriever outperforms generic graphics every time. Budget $200–$500 for a talented Fiverr or 99designs designer who has done pet branding before — ask to see specific pet work in their portfolio.

The copy formula for your front panel: lead with the benefit (JOINT SUPPORT), follow with key ingredients (with Glucosamine, Chondroitin & Green-Lipped Mussel), close with your brand promise (No corn, wheat, or soy fillers). Three seconds is all you get. Give them exactly those three signals.

Pricing Strategy: How to Capture 8–12 Extra Margin Points

Here's the math that makes private label work. Assume a competing national brand soft chew supplement retails at $32. You stock it and retail it at $28 to compete. At national brand wholesale pricing, your gross margin is 20–25%. Your landed cost per unit: roughly $21–22.

Your private label version of the same stock formula: if you manufacture 500 units at $8–10 per unit all-in (product + packaging), you retail at $28–32 and capture 65–70% gross margin. Every 100 units of your private label earns you the equivalent margin of selling 280–350 units of the national brand.

The pricing strategy that works: launch at a 10–15% discount below the category leader to invite trial. Accumulate 20–30 verified reviews. Then raise price to parity with the leading national brand at the 6-month mark. Your reviews and your customers' loyalty will hold the price. Private label cat food in North America is already achieving 25–35% profit margins at parity pricing — the same is within reach for supplements.

Your Launch Plan: Getting the First 100 Orders

A private label product launch doesn't need a big marketing budget. It needs the right sequence.

Email your existing customers first. Your email list is your lowest-cost, highest-trust distribution channel. Send a single "Introducing Our House Brand" email — tell the story of why you made it, name the co-packer's certifications, and offer 20% off the first order as an introductory price. A list of 2,000 engaged pet owner subscribers should convert 3–5% on a well-written launch email — that's 60–100 orders without spending a dollar on ads.

Bundle it as a trial with your bestsellers. Add your new private label supplement as a free sample (single-serve sachet or 7-count trial pack) with any order over $45. The customer who tries it and sees results becomes your most enthusiastic evangelist.

Write one piece of launch content. A 600-word blog post titled "Why We Created Our Own [Product]" outperforms any ad at this stage. It builds trust before the customer opens the bag, names your co-packer's credentials, and converts organic search traffic for months after publication. This is SEO equity that a national brand ad spend can't replicate.

Chase your first reviews hard. Personally email the first 20–30 buyers and ask for honest feedback. Offer a discount on their next order for a verified review. Those early reviews compound directly into organic search visibility — Google surfaces products with reviews, and pet shoppers read them obsessively before buying anything new.

Stack Private Label With Autoship for Maximum LTV

Here's the move that turns a solid private label launch into a recurring revenue machine: make your house brand available at its best price only through your autoship program.

One-time price: $32. Autoship price: $27.20 (15% off). That discount is entirely funded by your higher private label margins — you're still capturing 50%+ gross margin on the autoship order. And customers who subscribe for your private label supplement come back every 30–60 days on autopilot. Research consistently shows autoship customers carry 2–3x the lifetime value of one-time buyers, with lower churn and higher basket size as they add on other products to justify the convenience.

Your private label product becomes the anchor that locks subscribers into your ecosystem. Every autoship order that starts with your hip-and-joint chews is an order where you also see dental chews, food, and toys added. That's the compounding effect of a house brand done right — and it's a flywheel that Chewy cannot replicate because they can't build the trust relationship a dedicated indie store can.

The $54 billion private label pet food market isn't going to smaller stores by accident. It's going there because store owners realized they'd been giving away their margin to national brands for years. You now have the playbook. The MOQs are lower than you thought, the co-packers are better than you imagined, and your customers are more ready than you know.

The Bottom Line

Private label pet products aren't a future opportunity — they're a right-now opportunity. National brand unit sales declined while store brands grew. Your co-packer call costs you nothing. Your first 500-unit run of private label supplements might cost you $5,000 all-in, and a single successful product can add five or six points to your overall store margin for years.

Start with one supplement. Nail the quality. Tell the story. Price it right. Put it on autoship. Then build the next product from the revenue the first one generates.
