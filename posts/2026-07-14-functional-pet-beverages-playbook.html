Bone Broth, Electrolyte Water, and Goat Milk: How to Own the $11B Functional Pet Beverage Category Before Your Competitors Wake Up

📅 July 14, 2026 ✍️ PetStore.com Editorial Team 🏷️ Nutrition · Supplements · Hydration

Most pet store owners think "hydration" means making sure there's a water bowl on the floor. Here's what that misses: the pet beverage market — functional broths, electrolyte waters, powdered goat milk, and drinkable supplements — was valued at $3.78 billion in 2024 and is projected to hit $11.29 billion by 2033 at a 12.8% CAGR. That's one of the fastest-growing categories in all of pet care, and most independent stores are barely touching it. The stores that own this shelf in the next 12 months are going to look very smart very quickly.

The Dehydration Problem Pet Owners Don't Know They Have

Veterinary nutritionists have been saying it for years: most pets — especially cats, but a significant portion of dogs too — are chronically mildly dehydrated. Dry kibble diets, hot summers, and finicky drinkers all contribute. And plain water, while necessary, doesn't address the underlying functional gaps: electrolyte imbalances after exercise or heat stress, gut microbiome support, joint lubrication, or recovery nutrition after illness.

That's the entry point for functional pet beverages. It's not a gimmick. These products fill a genuine nutrition gap that millions of pet owners didn't know existed until they tried one and watched their dog slurp an entire bowl in seconds. That first "my dog never drinks this fast" moment is your word-of-mouth engine.

The summer timing isn't incidental, either. July and August are when dehydration risk peaks, vet visits for heat-related illness spike, and pet owners are most receptive to anything that protects their animal. Right now is the moment to get this category on your shelves and in your email flows.

The Four Sub-Categories You Need to Stock

Functional pet beverages aren't one product — they're a family of formats, each with its own use case and customer.

Bone broth is the entry point. Familiar to human wellness shoppers, easy to explain, and versatile — poured over kibble as a topper, frozen into enrichment lick pads, or served as a standalone drink. The category's most established player, Brutus Bone Broth, sells at roughly $10 for 32 ounces and has expanded into more than 15,000 retail locations including Petco and PetSmart. That's your benchmark. Open Farm and The Honest Kitchen also carry well-regarded broth lines with human-grade ingredients that indie stores can stock without competing directly on price with the big box chains.

Electrolyte waters and functional drinks are the fastest-moving sub-category right now. PAWP Water — presented at Global Pet Expo 2026 — sells in resealable 12 oz cans and comes in four formulations: electrolyte blend (potassium, sodium, magnesium), gut health with prebiotics, calming with tryptophan, and hip-and-joint with collagen. That's a cross-sell into four separate customer problems from a single shelf position. DoggyRade, formulated with amino acids and isotonic hydration, targets the active dog segment and markets heavily to sporting and agility communities. Aqualapka just launched their premium pet hydration line in the U.S. in 2026, so the category is actively expanding.

Goat milk — fresh, frozen, and powdered — is having a genuine moment. It's naturally rich in electrolytes, probiotics, and easily digestible proteins, and it's one of the most palatable formats you'll ever put in front of a picky eater or a recovering pet. Primal Pet Foods' powdered goat milk and Rogue Pet Science's G.O.A.T Milk (human-grade A2 protein, one 16 oz bag makes 6 quarts) are proven sellers. This format also works as a food topper, so it bridges your hydration category with your topper category — one product, two use cases, two merchandising angles.

Liquid and powder supplement drizzles round out the category. Native Pet offers vet-formulated broth powders with grass-fed beef and chicken. Finnfair's fish oil comes in a drizzle-style dispensing package designed for controlled portion use. These aren't "drinks" exactly — they're functional hydration delivery systems that enhance both palatability and nutrition simultaneously.

Brands Worth Stocking (and the Ones to Watch)

Here's a prioritized list by channel fit for independent stores:

  • Brutus Bone Broth — broad appeal, accessible price point (~$10/32 oz), made with human-grade ingredients and added glucosamine. Fast-moving, easy to explain. Good entry point for customers who've never bought a pet beverage.
  • PAWP Water — the most innovative brand in the space right now. Multi-SKU lineup lets you build a small section rather than a single facing. Functional claims (electrolytes, gut, calm, joint) each speak to a different customer need. Stock all four to maximize basket size.
  • Open Farm Bone Broth — premium positioning, traceable sourcing, loyal customer base. If you're already carrying Open Farm food, this is an effortless category extension. Cross-merchandised next to their kibble or raw toppers, attachment rate is high.
  • Rogue Pet Science G.O.A.T Milk — highest value density per shelf slot (one bag = 6 quarts of liquid). Subscription-friendly format, because a bag a month is a natural cadence. Strong attachment with raw feeders.
  • Native Pet Bone Broth Powder — vet-formulated with a direct-to-consumer following that will appreciate finding it in a specialty store. Great candidate for a "why we carry this" content angle.
  • DoggyRade — niche fit for stores with active, sporting, or outdoor-focused customer bases. If your customers are hiking with their dogs, training agility, or running in summer heat, this is your electrolyte angle.

Pricing, Margins, and the Math That Makes This Worth It

This category sits comfortably in supplement pricing territory, not commodity food territory. Ready-to-serve broths typically retail between $8–$14 for 32 oz. Functional canned beverages like PAWP Water retail for $12–$16 for a 6-pack. Powdered goat milk runs $18–$28 per bag. These are not price-sensitive shoppers — they are premium nutrition seekers who budget for their pet's health the same way they budget for their own.

Specialty pet stores typically see 40–55% gross margins on supplement and functional nutrition products — meaningfully higher than commodity kibble. And because the products are consumable on a monthly cadence, they're natural autoship candidates with strong reorder rates once a pet accepts the flavor.

The powder formats are particularly strong on shipping economics: lightweight, no refrigeration, no breakage risk, and high value-to-weight ratio. That matters for online stores where heavy food products can eat margin in fulfillment costs.

How to Merchandise This Category for Maximum Attachment

The biggest mistake stores make with functional beverages is burying them in a generic supplement section. This category needs context and education to sell — not because it's complicated, but because most customers don't know they need it until they understand what it does.

Three merchandising moves that work:

  • Cross-sell with food toppers. A customer buying a freeze-dried raw topper or a goat milk topper is already thinking about enhancing their pet's bowl. A "complete the bowl" product bundle — topper + bone broth — naturally lifts AOV by $10–15 per transaction.
  • Build a "Summer Hydration" collection. In July and August, a themed landing page or email series around hydration makes the category contextually obvious. "Your dog is working harder in this heat — here's how to keep them healthy" is a story that sells itself.
  • Use video and sampling to break the "my pet won't drink that" objection. The most effective content for this category is reaction videos — dogs going absolutely feral for a bowl of broth or goat milk. UGC from your own customers is gold here. Ask buyers to film their dog's first taste. Repost everything.

On your product pages, lead with the functional benefit, not the ingredient list. "Supports joint health and recovery" sells better than "contains glucosamine." The ingredients are proof points, not headlines.

The Email and SEO Opportunity Your Competitors Are Missing

Most indie pet stores still don't have a single piece of content about pet hydration beyond a generic "make sure your dog drinks water in summer" social post. That's a gap you can close with one well-written guide and watch it compound in organic search for years.

Target long-tail queries like "bone broth for dogs benefits," "how to rehydrate a sick dog," "best electrolyte drinks for active dogs," and "can dogs drink goat milk" — these have strong commercial intent and very little authoritative competition from independent pet stores. A 1,200-word guide on each ranks faster than you'd expect.

In your Klaviyo flows, add a post-purchase email for any food or topper purchase: "Your dog's bowl is almost perfect — here's the one thing that takes it from good to great." Link to your bone broth or goat milk collection. That's an incremental $12–20 per customer on an email that costs nothing to send.

Why This Category Is Built for Autoship

Everything about functional pet beverages makes them ideal for subscription revenue. They're consumable on a predictable schedule (a bag of goat milk powder every 3-4 weeks, a 6-pack of PAWP Water every 2-3 weeks). They're affordable enough that customers don't hesitate to automate. And once a picky pet decides they love bone broth poured over their food, that behavior becomes part of the daily routine — and the store that supplies it gets a very sticky customer.

Offer a 15% autoship discount on your functional beverage collection and position it as "your pet's daily hydration routine, handled." For customers already on an autoship for food or supplements, adding a beverage SKU is a low-friction upsell that meaningfully increases your per-subscriber LTV without requiring any acquisition spend.

The Bottom Line

The pet beverage category is where the pet supplement boom meets the hydration trend meets summer urgency. That's a rare convergence of macro tailwinds. The market is growing at 12.8% CAGR toward $11 billion. The customer appetite is there. The brands are ready to wholesale. What's missing is independent stores that understand this isn't a novelty category — it's the next supplement.

Stock six to eight SKUs across bone broth, electrolyte water, and goat milk. Cross-merchandise with your topper and food sections. Build one summer hydration email flow. That's a few hours of work and a category that could be generating $2,000–5,000/month in recurring revenue before the summer is out.
